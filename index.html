Defining global cities

The world's largest city regions concentrate the drivers of global prosperity, but there isn't one way to be a global city—there are seven.

  1. Urbanization means cities matter for the global economy more than any time in recent history.
  2. Globalization means the firms and institutions in cities need to be outward looking, focused on trade, FDI, and international labor markets.
  3. Technological change means these same firms and institutions must focus on maximizing productivity through innovation, cultivating skilled labor, and enhancing digital infrastructure.
  4. Cities are heterogeneous. They are meeting the challenges of globalization and technological change in different ways. We find seven types of global cities.

Factory China (22 metropolitan areas)

Second and third-tier Chinese cities distinctly reliant on export-intensive, manufacturing to power economic growth and global engagement.

Knowledge Capitals (19 metropolitan areas)

Mid-sized, highly productive knowledge creation centers in the United States and Europe with talented workforces and elite research universities.

Emerging Gateways (28 metro areas)

Large business and transportation entry points for major national and regional emerging markets in Africa, Asia, Eastern Europe, and Latin America.

Asian Anchors (6 metro areas)

Large, business and financial nodes anchoring inward investment into Asia.

Global Giants (6 metro areas)

Large, wealthy financial hubs that serve as the command and control centers for the world’s largest advanced economies.

American Middleweights (16 metro areas)

Mid-sized U.S. metro areas striving for a post-recession niche in the global economy that must rely on anchor assets to propel transitions to more productive growth.

Middling Magnets (26 metro areas)

Mid-sized cities in Australia, Canada, and Europe globally connected by people and investment flows, but where growth has lagged after the financial crisis.
